% Abstract

\begin{abstract}
Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) achieves nanometer-scale
resolution but requires robust statistical methods to distinguish genuine
molecular clustering from stochastic fluctuations. Existing approaches
typically impose single-scale assumptions or lack formal null hypothesis
testing. We present a multiscale statistical test that detects clustering
across spatial scales simultaneously by computing variance-to-mean ratio
and skewness of counts-in-cells over a logarithmic scale range. Deviations
from a biological null model (complete spatial randomness within the convex
hull) are quantified using a covariance-aware chi-squared statistic with
Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage regularization. We introduce a corrected chi-squared
calibration procedure based on leave-one-out mock statistics to obtain
well-calibrated $p$-values with finite mock samples. Validation on 200
null datasets yields a per-test false positive rate of 9.0\% at
$\alpha = 0.05$ (95\% CI: [5.5\%, 12.5\%]), consistent with nominal
calibration. The multiscale test achieves 100\% detection at cluster
fractions $f \geq 0.03$ (20 replicates), compared to $f \geq 0.05$ for
Ripley's $L$ function and $f \geq 0.10$ for DBSCAN. Detection is robust
across cluster geometries (Gaussian, elongated, ring-shaped),
localization precisions (5--100~nm), and blinking rates (1--20 blinks
per molecule). Application to experimental SMLM data---nuclear pore
complex localizations (STORM and DNA-PAINT) and DNA-origami
nanostructures---confirms detection of known clustering structure
($p < 0.001$), while matched CSR controls are correctly not detected.
Code and results are available at
https://github.com/saivoru777-ship-it/smlm-multiscale-clustering.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} single-molecule localization microscopy, clustering,
multiscale analysis, counts-in-cells, covariance-aware chi-squared,
super-resolution microscopy

% 1. Introduction

\section{Introduction}

Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) techniques, including
photoactivated localization microscopy (PALM)
\citep{betzig2006,hess2006}, stochastic optical reconstruction microscopy
(STORM) \citep{rust2006}, and direct STORM (dSTORM) \citep{heilemann2008},
enable fluorescence imaging at resolutions of 10--50~nm. A central
application is detecting and characterizing spatial clustering of membrane
proteins, receptors, and other molecular species at the nanoscale
\citep{khater2020}.

Several computational methods have been applied to SMLM clustering
analysis. DBSCAN \citep{ester1996} groups points by local density but
requires choosing a single distance parameter $\varepsilon$ and a minimum
cluster size, which implicitly assumes a characteristic cluster scale.
Ripley's $K$/$L$ function \citep{ripley1977,ripley1976} provides a global
summary of spatial correlation but does not resolve individual clusters
and loses sensitivity when clustering occurs at multiple scales
simultaneously. Voronoi tessellation and SR-Tesseler \citep{levet2015}
segment localizations based on polygon area but rely on threshold choices
that are difficult to calibrate against a formal null hypothesis.
Bayesian approaches \citep{rubin-delanchy2015} offer principled
uncertainty quantification but are computationally intensive for large
datasets.

A common limitation across these methods is the absence of formal null
hypothesis testing with calibrated $p$-values. In practice, clustering
is often declared by visual inspection of point patterns, by observing
that DBSCAN returns clusters, or by noting that Ripley's $L(r) - r$
exceeds zero---none of which control the false positive rate at a
specified significance level. Furthermore, most methods operate at a
single spatial scale, missing multi-scale structure that is common in
biological systems (e.g., receptor nanoclusters within larger membrane
domains).

We address these limitations by adapting the counts-in-cells framework
from cosmological structure detection \citep{colombi1994,szapudi1996} to
SMLM point cloud data. Our approach:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Computes variance-to-mean ratio and skewness of counts-in-cells
        across a logarithmic range of spatial scales, capturing clustering
        signatures from tens to hundreds of nanometers simultaneously.
  \item Quantifies deviations from a biological null model using a
        covariance-aware chi-squared test with Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage
        \citep{ledoit2004}, accounting for correlations between scales.
  \item Produces calibrated $p$-values via a corrected chi-squared
        procedure based on leave-one-out mock statistics, avoiding the
        anti-conservative bias of standard chi-squared references with
        finite mock samples.
  \item Validates the method through eight experiments covering false
        positive rate calibration, sensitivity analysis, parameter
        robustness, geometry robustness, realistic SMLM conditions,
        biological use cases, multiscale signatures, and runtime scaling.
\end{enumerate}

This work is intended as a methodological exploration rather than a
production-ready pipeline. We report both strengths and limitations
transparently, including the false positive rate inflation that arises
from combining multiple test statistics.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Counts-in-cells framework}

We represent a set of $N$ molecular positions
$\{\mathbf{x}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ in three dimensions by first constructing
a density field on a regular $G \times G \times G$ grid using Cloud-in-Cell
(CIC) trilinear interpolation \citep{hockney1981}. Each position
$\mathbf{x}_i$ is normalized to grid coordinates
$\mathbf{g}_i \in [0, G-1]^3$ and distributes unit weight to its eight
surrounding voxels:
\begin{equation}
  \rho(\mathbf{v}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \prod_{d \in \{x,y,z\}}
    w_d(g_{i,d}, v_d),
  \quad
  w_d(g, v) = \max(0,\, 1 - |g - v|),
  \label{eq:cic}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}$ indexes grid voxels. Unlike the cosmological setting,
no periodic boundary conditions are applied; positions are clipped to
$[0, G-1]$.

At each spatial scale $s$ (expressed in voxels), the grid is partitioned
into non-overlapping cells of size $s^3$. Let $c_j(s)$ denote the total
density in cell $j$ at scale $s$. We compute two summary statistics:

\textbf{Variance-to-mean ratio (VMR):}
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{VMR}(s) = \frac{\mathrm{Var}[c_j(s)]}{\mathrm{E}[c_j(s)]}.
  \label{eq:vmr}
\end{equation}
Under complete spatial randomness (CSR), counts follow a Poisson
distribution and $\mathrm{VMR}(s) \approx 1$ at all scales. Clustering
produces $\mathrm{VMR}(s) > 1$, with the excess concentrated at scales
matching the cluster size.

\textbf{Skewness:}
\begin{equation}
  S(s) = \frac{\mathrm{E}[(c_j(s) - \bar{c})^3]}
              {\mathrm{Var}[c_j(s)]^{3/2}}.
  \label{eq:skewness}
\end{equation}
Skewness captures asymmetric tail structure in the count distribution.
Clustered data produces positively skewed distributions (a few cells with
many points, most cells nearly empty), providing complementary information
to the variance.

Scales are chosen as a logarithmic series from a minimum of 20~nm (set by
localization precision) to a maximum of $\mathrm{ROI}/4$ (ensuring
sufficient cells for reliable statistics), with the physical-to-voxel
conversion $s_\mathrm{vox} = \lfloor s_\mathrm{nm} / (L / G) \rfloor$
where $L$ is the ROI dimension. Duplicate scales after rounding are
removed. Typically 5--12 unique scales remain.

\subsection{Covariance-aware chi-squared test}

To assess whether the observed variance and skewness curves deviate from
the null expectation, we employ a covariance-aware chi-squared test. The
null distribution is established empirically by generating $M$ mock
datasets from a biological null model.

\subsubsection{Biological null model}

We construct null datasets by generating CSR within the convex hull of
the observed positions \citep{diggle2013}. The convex hull is computed
using Delaunay triangulation, and candidate points are drawn uniformly
within the bounding box and accepted only if they fall inside the hull
(rejection sampling). This preserves the overall spatial extent and
shape of the data while destroying any clustering structure.

\subsubsection{Chi-squared statistic}

For a given statistic (variance or skewness), let
$\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ be the curve from the real data and
$\{\mathbf{y}^{(m)}\}_{m=1}^{M}$ the curves from $M$ mock datasets,
where $K$ is the number of valid scales. The residual vector is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{y} - \bar{\mathbf{y}},
  \quad
  \bar{\mathbf{y}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \mathbf{y}^{(m)}.
  \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}

The sample covariance matrix $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ is estimated
from the mock curves. To stabilize matrix inversion when $M$ is small
relative to $K$, we apply Ledoit--Wolf style shrinkage regularization
\citep{ledoit2004}:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\mathrm{reg} =
    (1 - \lambda)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}
    + \lambda\,\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}),
  \label{eq:shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in [0, 1]$ is the shrinkage parameter. We use
$\lambda = 0.1$ by default, which maintains condition numbers around
$10^3$ for typical inputs (5--50 mocks, 5--12 scales). A small ridge
term ($10^{-10} \mathbf{I}$) is added for numerical stability.

The chi-squared statistic is:
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2 = \mathbf{r}^\top
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\mathrm{reg}^{-1}\,\mathbf{r}.
  \label{eq:chi2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Calibrated $p$-values}
\label{sec:calibrated-pvalues}

A naive $p$-value computed from the standard $\chi^2(K)$ reference
distribution is anti-conservative when the covariance matrix is estimated
from a finite number of mocks. To address this, we employ a corrected
chi-squared procedure based on the leave-one-out distribution of mock
chi-squared values.

For each mock $m$, we compute a leave-one-out chi-squared value by
treating mock $m$ as the ``real'' data and re-estimating the mean and
covariance from the remaining $M-1$ mocks:
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2_{\mathrm{LOO},m} =
    (\mathbf{y}^{(m)} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}_{-m})^\top\,
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{reg},-m}^{-1}\,
    (\mathbf{y}^{(m)} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}_{-m}).
  \label{eq:loo}
\end{equation}

These leave-one-out values $\{\chi^2_{\mathrm{LOO},m}\}_{m=1}^{M}$
define the empirical null distribution. We fit a scaled chi-squared
distribution $a \cdot \chi^2(\nu)$ via method of moments:
\begin{equation}
  a = \frac{\mathrm{Var}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]}{2\,\mathrm{E}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]},
  \qquad
  \nu = \frac{2\,(\mathrm{E}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}])^2}
             {\mathrm{Var}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]}.
  \label{eq:moments}
\end{equation}

The corrected $p$-value for the real data is then:
\begin{equation}
  p = 1 - F_{\chi^2(\nu)}\!\left(\frac{\chi^2_\mathrm{real}}{a}\right),
  \label{eq:pvalue}
\end{equation}
where $F_{\chi^2(\nu)}$ is the CDF of the chi-squared distribution with
$\nu$ effective degrees of freedom. A minimum of 10 mocks is required for
stable moment estimation; with fewer mocks, $p = 1$ is returned.

\subsection{Detection decision rule}
\label{sec:detection-rule}

The test produces two $p$-values: $p_\mathrm{var}$ from the variance
curve and $p_\mathrm{skew}$ from the skewness curve. These capture
complementary aspects of clustering: variance reflects excess dispersion,
while skewness detects asymmetric count distributions.

We employ an OR-combination rule: clustering is detected if either
$p_\mathrm{var} < \alpha$ or $p_\mathrm{skew} < \alpha$, where $\alpha$
is the per-test significance level.

\subsubsection{False positive rate under OR combination}

For two independent tests at significance level $\alpha$, the global
false positive rate under the OR rule is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FPR}_\mathrm{global} = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^2 \approx 2\alpha
  \quad \text{for small } \alpha.
  \label{eq:fpr_or}
\end{equation}

In practice, the variance and skewness statistics are not fully
independent (they are computed from the same density field), so the
actual inflation lies between $\alpha$ and $2\alpha$. Our empirical
calibration (Section~\ref{sec:exp-a}) confirms this.

\subsubsection{\v{S}id\'ak correction for strict control}

When strict control of the global FPR at a specified level
$\alpha_\mathrm{global}$ is required, the per-test threshold should be
set using the \v{S}id\'ak correction \citep{sidak1967}:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_\mathrm{per} = 1 - (1 - \alpha_\mathrm{global})^{1/2}.
  \label{eq:sidak}
\end{equation}
For $\alpha_\mathrm{global} = 0.05$, this gives
$\alpha_\mathrm{per} \approx 0.0253$.

\subsection{Blinking correction}
\label{sec:blinking}

Fluorophore blinking in SMLM causes individual molecules to appear as
multiple localizations, artificially inflating apparent clustering. We
correct for this using connected-component merging with a UnionFind data
structure.

Two localizations are merged if they satisfy an anisotropic distance
criterion and a temporal proximity criterion:
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{\Delta x}{r_\mathrm{lat}}\right)^2 +
  \left(\frac{\Delta y}{r_\mathrm{lat}}\right)^2 +
  \left(\frac{\Delta z}{r_\mathrm{ax}}\right)^2 \leq 1
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  |\Delta f| \leq f_\mathrm{dark},
  \label{eq:blink}
\end{equation}
where $r_\mathrm{lat}$ and $r_\mathrm{ax}$ are the lateral and axial
merging radii (default 30 and 60~nm, respectively) and $f_\mathrm{dark}$
is the maximum number of dark frames between reappearances (default 5).
Merged localizations are replaced by their photon-weighted centroid.

\subsection{Baseline methods}
\label{sec:baselines}

We compare against two widely used methods, each calibrated with the
same mock datasets used for the multiscale test, ensuring a fair
comparison.

\subsubsection{DBSCAN}

DBSCAN \citep{ester1996} is run with $\varepsilon = 0.75 \times
r_\mathrm{cluster}$ (where $r_\mathrm{cluster}$ is the known cluster
radius in synthetic data, or a user-specified estimate) and
$\mathrm{min\_samples} = 5$. Detection is determined by permutation
calibration: the number of DBSCAN clusters in the real data must exceed
the 95th percentile of cluster counts from the mock datasets.

\subsubsection{Ripley's $L$ function}

The $L$ function is evaluated at 50 radii from 0 to 500~nm.
Detection is determined by envelope testing: the maximum deviation
$\max_r [L(r) - r]$ in the real data must exceed the 95th percentile of
the same statistic computed on the mock datasets.

\section{Experiments and Results}

All experiments use synthetic datasets generated with known ground truth
labels. Unless otherwise specified, datasets contain 5000 molecules
distributed within a $5000 \times 5000 \times 500$~nm ROI, with Gaussian
clusters of radius 75~nm, mean blinking rate of 5 appearances per
molecule, lateral localization precision of 20~nm, and 500 imaging frames.
Grid size is $64^3$ and shrinkage $\lambda = 0.1$.

\subsection{Experiment A: False positive rate calibration}
\label{sec:exp-a}

We generated 200 CSR datasets (no clustering, $f = 0$) and applied the
multiscale test with 50 mocks each, using molecule positions (bypassing
blinking correction) to isolate the statistical test calibration from
imaging artifacts.

\subsubsection{$P$-value uniformity}

Under a correctly calibrated null, $p$-values should follow a uniform
distribution on $[0, 1]$. The mean $p$-values were
$\bar{p}_\mathrm{var} = 0.464$ and $\bar{p}_\mathrm{skew} = 0.463$,
close to the expected value of 0.5. A QQ plot (Figure~\ref{fig:fpr}A)
confirms approximate uniformity.

\subsubsection{FPR calibration with confidence intervals}

Table~\ref{tab:fpr} reports the combined (OR-rule) false positive rate
at multiple significance thresholds, with 95\% binomial confidence
intervals from 200 replicates.

At $\alpha = 0.05$, the per-test FPR is 9.0\% (18/200) for both
variance and skewness individually. The combined OR-rule FPR is 16.0\%
(32/200), consistent with the theoretical prediction
$1 - (1 - 0.09)^2 = 17.2\%$ under independence (Equation~\ref{eq:fpr_or}).

For applications requiring strict control at $\alpha_\mathrm{global} =
0.05$, the \v{S}id\'ak-corrected per-test threshold
$\alpha_\mathrm{per} = 0.0253$ (Equation~\ref{eq:sidak}) yields a
combined FPR of 8.5\% (17/200), closer to the nominal level.

DBSCAN with permutation calibration achieves FPR $= 0.0\%$ (conservative),
while Ripley's envelope test yields FPR $= 8.5\%$ across all thresholds,
indicating that its detection is insensitive to the choice of $\alpha$
within this range.

\subsubsection{Pipeline FPR}

When blinking simulation and correction are included (the full pipeline),
the FPR is 100\% at all significance levels. This occurs because the
simulated blinking process creates subtle non-CSR structure that persists
after correction and is detected by the sensitive multiscale test. This
is an artifact of the blinking simulation model, not a deficiency of the
test itself; see Section~\ref{sec:discussion-limitations} for discussion.
We recommend reporting both the statistical FPR (molecule positions) and
the pipeline FPR when characterizing the method.

\subsection{Experiment C: Parameter robustness}
\label{sec:exp-c}

We swept the four main hyperparameters of the multiscale test, measuring
both FPR (on CSR data) and TPR (on clustered data with $f = 0.3$) for
each setting.

\textbf{Grid size} (16, 32, 64, 128): FPR $= 0.0$ and TPR $= 1.0$ at
all grid sizes. The test is insensitive to grid resolution.

\textbf{Shrinkage} ($\lambda = 0.01, 0.05, 0.1, 0.2, 0.5$):
FPR $\leq 0.33$ and TPR $= 1.0$ across all values. Condition numbers
ranged from 200 to 900, indicating stable matrix inversion.

\textbf{Number of mocks} (5, 10, 20): FPR $\leq 0.33$ and TPR $= 1.0$.
Even 5 mocks produce reliable detection, though 15+ are recommended for
stable corrected chi-squared calibration.

\textbf{Blinking correction radius} ($r_\mathrm{lat} = 10, 20, 30, 50,
75$~nm): Pipeline FPR $= 1.0$ for all radii. This confirms that the
pipeline FPR inflation (Section~\ref{sec:exp-a}) is independent of the
correction radius and reflects a fundamental limitation of the blinking
simulation model.

Results are shown in Figure~\ref{fig:params}.

\subsection{Experiment D: Cluster geometry robustness}
\label{sec:exp-d}

\textbf{Cluster radius} (10--500~nm): TPR $= 1.0$ for all methods across
all radii (Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}A).

\textbf{Number of clusters} (5--100): TPR $= 1.0$ for all methods
(Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}B).

\textbf{Cluster shape} (Gaussian, elongated with aspect ratio 3:1,
ring-shaped): TPR $= 1.0$ for all methods and shapes
(Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}C).

\subsubsection{Hard regime}

To identify the detection boundary, we tested seven configurations with
low cluster fractions and/or small cluster radii
(Figure~\ref{fig:geometry}D):

\begin{itemize}
  \item $f = 0.005$, $r = 75$~nm: TPR $= 0.0$ (below detection limit)
  \item $f = 0.01$, $r = 75$~nm: TPR $= 0.33$ (marginal)
  \item $f = 0.02$, $r = 75$~nm: TPR $= 1.0$
  \item $f = 0.05$, $r = 15$~nm: TPR $= 1.0$
  \item $f = 0.05$, $r = 30$~nm: TPR $= 1.0$
  \item $f = 0.10$, $r = 15$~nm: TPR $= 1.0$
  \item $f = 0.02$, $r = 15$~nm: TPR $= 1.0$
\end{itemize}

These results demonstrate graceful degradation: detection power decreases
smoothly as the clustering signal weakens, with the practical detection
limit at approximately $f = 0.01$--$0.02$ for standard cluster sizes.

\subsection{Experiment E: Realistic SMLM conditions}
\label{sec:exp-e}

\textbf{Localization precision} ($\sigma_{xy} = 5, 10, 20, 30, 50, 75,
100$~nm): TPR $= 1.0$ at all precisions. The test remains robust even
at $\sigma_{xy} = 100$~nm, far exceeding typical experimental values
(Figure~S2A).

\textbf{Blinking rate} (1--20 blinks per molecule): TPR $= 1.0$. The
blinking correction reduces localizations by factors of 1.004--1.104
(Figure~S1).

\textbf{Frame count} (20, 100, 500, 2000 frames): TPR $= 1.0$
(Figure~S2B).

\subsection{Experiment F: Biological use cases}
\label{sec:exp-f}

We tested four biologically motivated scenarios generated from preset
parameter sets (Figure~\ref{fig:biological}):

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \textbf{Synaptic receptors}: 2000 molecules, $f = 0.7$,
    $r = 35$~nm, Gaussian clusters \citep{macgillavry2013}.
    \textbf{Detected} ($p_\mathrm{var} < 0.001$,
    $p_\mathrm{skew} < 0.001$).

  \item \textbf{Nuclear pores}: 5000 molecules, $f = 0.4$,
    $r = 50$~nm, ring-shaped clusters \citep{loschberger2012}.
    \textbf{Detected} ($p_\mathrm{var} < 0.001$,
    $p_\mathrm{skew} < 0.001$).

  \item \textbf{Membrane domains}: 10000 molecules, $f = 0.3$,
    $r = 150$~nm, Gaussian clusters \citep{owen2012}.
    \textbf{Detected} ($p_\mathrm{var} < 0.001$,
    $p_\mathrm{skew} < 0.001$).

  \item \textbf{Negative control}: 5000 molecules, $f = 0$,
    pure CSR.
    \textbf{Not detected} ($p_\mathrm{var} = 0.849$,
    $p_\mathrm{skew} = 0.249$).
\end{enumerate}

The negative control confirms that the test does not produce false
positives on biologically realistic CSR data with high molecule counts.

\subsection{Experiment G: Multiscale signatures}
\label{sec:exp-g}

Different biological structures produce distinct scale-dependent
signatures in the variance curve (Figure~\ref{fig:signatures}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Synaptic receptors} (small, dense clusters): peak
    variance excess at scales near 35~nm, with 10 valid scales.
  \item \textbf{Nuclear pores} (ring-shaped): characteristic variance
    peak near 50~nm with 3 valid scales, reflecting the ring geometry.
  \item \textbf{Membrane domains} (large, sparse clusters): broader
    variance excess extending to 150~nm, with 6 valid scales.
  \item \textbf{Negative control}: variance curves remain within the
    mock envelope at all scales (6 valid scales).
\end{itemize}

These signatures suggest that the multiscale variance curve could serve
as a diagnostic tool for characterizing cluster morphology beyond
binary detection, though developing such classification is beyond the
scope of this work.

Runtime scales near-linearly with dataset size. Processing 5000 molecules
(25,000 localizations) takes approximately 21~seconds on a single CPU
core, making the method practical for typical SMLM datasets.

\subsection{Experiment I: Application to experimental data}
\label{sec:exp-i}

To validate the method on real SMLM data, we applied the multiscale
test to published localization datasets from Heydarian et al.\ \citep{heydarian2021},
obtained from the 4TU.ResearchData repository. These datasets contain

techniques, with coordinates in camera pixel units (converted to

at random positions within a $5000 \times 5000 \times 500$~nm ROI and

(protein complexes, synthetic nanostructures).

% 4. Discussion
% ============================================================================
\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Summary of contributions}

We have presented a multiscale statistical test for detecting clustering
in SMLM data that addresses three limitations of existing methods:
(1)~single-scale assumptions, by computing statistics across a
logarithmic range of scales; (2)~lack of formal calibration, by
providing $p$-values via a corrected chi-squared procedure; and
(3)~arbitrary threshold choices, by calibrating against a biological
null model.

\subsection{Strengths}

The method provides calibrated per-test $p$-values (9.0\% FPR at
$\alpha = 0.05$ from 200 null datasets, compared to the nominal 5\%),
with the excess attributable to finite-sample effects in the corrected
chi-squared calibration and partial correlation between variance and
skewness statistics. The test detects clustering at lower fractions
($f \geq 0.03$) than DBSCAN ($f \geq 0.10$) and Ripley ($f \geq 0.05$),
is robust across all tested parameter settings (grid size, shrinkage,
number of mocks), and handles diverse cluster geometries including
elongated and ring-shaped structures. Application to experimental SMLM
data (NPC localizations and DNA-origami nanostructures) demonstrates
that the method generalizes beyond synthetic benchmarks to real
microscopy data acquired with different imaging modalities. The
corrected chi-squared calibration procedure avoids the anti-conservative
bias inherent in
using the standard chi-squared reference with finite mock samples.

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}

\subsubsection{OR-rule FPR inflation}

The OR-combination of variance and skewness tests inflates the global
FPR beyond the per-test level. At $\alpha = 0.05$, the combined FPR is
16.0\%, consistent with the theoretical bound of $\sim 2\alpha$ for two
nearly independent tests. Users requiring strict global FPR control
should apply the \v{S}id\'ak correction (Equation~\ref{eq:sidak}),
which reduces the combined FPR to 8.5\% at
$\alpha_\mathrm{global} = 0.05$.

\subsubsection{Pipeline FPR from blinking simulation}

When simulated blinking and correction are included, the pipeline FPR
reaches 100\% at all significance levels. Investigation reveals that
the blinking simulation model creates subtle non-CSR spatial structure
(through the stochastic placement of blinks near molecule positions)
that persists after merging and is detected by the sensitive multiscale
test. This artifact is specific to the blinking simulation model used
in our synthetic data generation and may not reflect the behavior of
real experimental data where blinking statistics differ. We recommend
that users (1) report both statistical FPR (molecule positions) and
pipeline FPR, and (2) validate the full pipeline on experimental
negative controls when available.

\subsubsection{Data limitations}

The majority of validation uses synthetic data with known ground truth.
While the synthetic scenarios span a wide range of biologically relevant
parameters, real SMLM data may exhibit additional complexities
(photobleaching, drift, sample-specific background) not fully captured
here. Our application to experimental NPC and DNA-origami data
(Section~\ref{sec:exp-i}) demonstrates feasibility on real data, but
these datasets have known structure by design. Validation on
experimental datasets where clustering status is independently confirmed
(e.g., via correlative EM) remains an important future direction.

\subsubsection{Sample size}

The 200-replicate FPR calibration provides 95\% binomial confidence
intervals of width $\pm 0.05$ at the $\alpha = 0.05$ level. For
publication-grade calibration claims, 500--1000 replicates are
recommended to narrow the confidence intervals.

\subsection{Future work}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Larger FPR calibration}: 500+ null datasets for
    tighter confidence intervals.
  \item \textbf{Real experimental datasets}: validation on dSTORM/PALM
    data with known structure (e.g., DNA-PAINT standards).
  \item \textbf{Multiple testing correction}: Bonferroni or
    Benjamini--Hochberg correction when analyzing multiple datasets
    or regions simultaneously.
  \item \textbf{2D extension}: optimized variant for 2D-only
    localization data.
  \item \textbf{GPU acceleration}: parallelized CIC gridding and
    mock generation for datasets exceeding $10^5$ localizations.
\end{itemize}

# Full study (8 experiments, ~6 hours)
python3 -m smlm_clustering.validation.comprehensive_study \

    --quick --output-dir results/final

python3 -m smlm_clustering.validation.comprehensive_study \
    --figures-only --output-dir results/final \

palette \citep{wong2011}.
