% Abstract
% ============================================================================
\begin{abstract}
Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) achieves nanometer-scale
resolution but requires robust statistical methods to distinguish genuine
molecular clustering from stochastic fluctuations. Existing approaches
typically impose single-scale assumptions, lack formal null hypothesis
testing, or require prior knowledge of cluster parameters. We present a
multiscale statistical framework that simultaneously detects clustering
and characterizes its spatial organization by computing variance-to-mean
ratio and skewness of counts-in-cells across a logarithmic scale range.
Deviations from a biological null model are quantified using a
covariance-aware chi-squared statistic with Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage
regularization. A corrected chi-squared calibration procedure based on
leave-one-out mock statistics yields well-calibrated $p$-values with
finite mock samples. Applied to experimental SMLM data---nuclear pore
complex localizations (STORM and DNA-PAINT) and DNA-origami
nanostructures---the method correctly detects all structured datasets
($p < 0.001$) while both CSR and permutation negative controls are
correctly not detected, outperforming HDBSCAN which misses two of three
structured datasets. Beyond binary detection, the multiscale variance
and skewness curves provide interpretable structural fingerprints:
different biological structures produce distinct scale-dependent
signatures, enabling characterization of nanoscale organization without
prior knowledge of cluster geometry or scale. Synthetic validation across
200 null datasets, 10 cluster fractions, diverse geometries, and
realistic imaging conditions confirms calibrated false positive rates and
superior sensitivity compared to DBSCAN, Ripley's $L$ function, and
HDBSCAN. Code and data are available at
\url{https://github.com/saivoru777-ship-it/smlm-multiscale-clustering}.
\end{abstract}

\textbf{Keywords:} single-molecule localization microscopy, clustering,
multiscale analysis, spatial statistics, structural fingerprints,
super-resolution microscopy

% 1. Introduction

\section{Introduction}

Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) techniques, including
photoactivated localization microscopy (PALM)
\citep{betzig2006,hess2006}, stochastic optical reconstruction microscopy
(STORM) \citep{rust2006}, and direct STORM (dSTORM) \citep{heilemann2008},
enable fluorescence imaging at resolutions of 10--50~nm. A central
application is detecting and characterizing spatial clustering of membrane
proteins, receptors, and other molecular species at the nanoscale
\citep{khater2020}.

\subsection{Existing methods and their limitations}

Several computational methods have been applied to SMLM clustering
analysis. \textbf{Density-based methods}---DBSCAN \citep{ester1996} and
its hierarchical extension HDBSCAN---group points by local density but
require choosing distance parameters ($\varepsilon$, minimum cluster size)
that implicitly assume a characteristic cluster scale. A recent
benchmarking study \citep{nieves2023} evaluated seven SMLM clustering
algorithms (DBSCAN, ToMATo, KDE, FOCAL, CAML, ClusterViSu, SR-Tesseler)
and found that the best performer depended on the underlying distribution
of localizations, with no single method dominating across all conditions.
\textbf{Ripley's $K$/$L$ function} \citep{ripley1977,ripley1976} provides
a global summary of spatial correlation but does not resolve individual
clusters and reduces to a single summary statistic.
\textbf{Voronoi-based methods}---SR-Tesseler \citep{levet2015} and
ClusterViSu---segment localizations by polygon area but rely on threshold
choices that are difficult to calibrate against a formal null hypothesis.
\textbf{Bayesian approaches} \citep{rubin-delanchy2015} offer principled
uncertainty quantification but are computationally intensive for large
datasets.

A common limitation is the absence of formal null hypothesis testing with
calibrated $p$-values. In practice, clustering is often declared by visual
inspection, by observing that DBSCAN returns clusters, or by noting that
Ripley's $L(r) - r$ exceeds zero---none of which control the false
positive rate at a specified significance level. Furthermore, most methods
operate at a single spatial scale, missing multi-scale structure common in
biological systems (e.g., receptor nanoclusters within larger membrane
domains).

\subsection{The detection--characterization distinction}

Existing SMLM clustering tools broadly fall into two categories:
\emph{cluster identification methods} (DBSCAN, SR-Tesseler, Bayesian
approaches) that output spatial assignments of localizations to clusters,
and \emph{global detection methods} (Ripley's $K$, pair correlation
function) that test whether clustering exists without localizing
individual clusters. Our method belongs to the second category. It
complements cluster-identification algorithms by providing a statistically
calibrated answer to a logically prior question: \emph{does clustering
exist at all, and at what spatial scales?} This test should be applied
before running segmentation methods, whose outputs are difficult to
interpret when the underlying data is consistent with complete spatial
randomness (CSR).

\subsection{Multiscale statistics as structural fingerprints}

Beyond binary detection, a key insight is that the scale-dependent
variance and skewness curves produced by our method encode structural
information about the underlying spatial organization. Different
biological structures---synaptic receptor nanoclusters, nuclear pore
complexes, membrane domains---produce distinct multiscale signatures that
act as fingerprints of nanoscale molecular organization. These correspond
to the second and third moments of the count distribution at each scale,
which together capture the dominant deviations from CSR: excess
dispersion (variance) and asymmetric tail structure (skewness). Higher
cumulants could capture additional structure but are less robust with
finite data.

\subsection{Contributions}

We address the limitations above by adapting the counts-in-cells
framework from cosmological structure detection
\citep{colombi1994,szapudi1996} to SMLM point cloud data. Our
contributions are:
\begin{enumerate}
  \item A multiscale statistical test computing variance and skewness of
        counts-in-cells across a logarithmic range of spatial scales,
        requiring no prior knowledge of cluster scale or geometry.
  \item Calibrated $p$-values via a corrected chi-squared procedure based
        on leave-one-out mock statistics, with covariance-aware
        Ledoit--Wolf shrinkage \citep{ledoit2004}.
  \item Demonstration that the multiscale variance and skewness curves
        serve as interpretable structural fingerprints of nanoscale
        organization.
  \item Comprehensive validation on experimental data (three imaging
        modalities, two negative controls) and synthetic benchmarks
        (nine experiments covering false positive rates, sensitivity,
        parameter robustness, geometry, imaging conditions, biological
        use cases, and runtime).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Counts-in-cells framework}

We represent a set of $N$ molecular positions
$\{\mathbf{x}_i\}_{i=1}^{N}$ in three dimensions by first constructing
a density field on a regular $G \times G \times G$ grid using Cloud-in-Cell
(CIC) trilinear interpolation \citep{hockney1981}. Each position
$\mathbf{x}_i$ is normalized to grid coordinates
$\mathbf{g}_i \in [0, G-1]^3$ and distributes unit weight to its eight
surrounding voxels:
\begin{equation}
  \rho(\mathbf{v}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \prod_{d \in \{x,y,z\}}
    w_d(g_{i,d}, v_d),
  \quad
  w_d(g, v) = \max(0,\, 1 - |g - v|),
  \label{eq:cic}
\end{equation}
where $\mathbf{v}$ indexes grid voxels. Unlike the cosmological setting,
no periodic boundary conditions are applied; positions are clipped to
$[0, G-1]$.

At each spatial scale $s$ (expressed in voxels), the grid is partitioned
into non-overlapping cells of size $s^3$. Let $c_j(s)$ denote the total
density in cell $j$ at scale $s$. We compute two summary statistics:

\textbf{Variance-to-mean ratio (VMR):}
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{VMR}(s) = \frac{\mathrm{Var}[c_j(s)]}{\mathrm{E}[c_j(s)]}.
  \label{eq:vmr}
\end{equation}
Under CSR, counts follow a Poisson distribution and
$\mathrm{VMR}(s) \approx 1$ at all scales. Clustering produces
$\mathrm{VMR}(s) > 1$, with the excess concentrated at scales matching
the cluster size. The scale at which VMR peaks, and the width of the
excess, encode information about the characteristic size and spatial
extent of the clustering structure.

\textbf{Skewness:}
\begin{equation}
  S(s) = \frac{\mathrm{E}[(c_j(s) - \bar{c})^3]}
              {\mathrm{Var}[c_j(s)]^{3/2}}.
  \label{eq:skewness}
\end{equation}
Skewness captures asymmetric tail structure in the count distribution.
Clustered data produces positively skewed distributions (a few cells with
many points, most cells nearly empty), providing complementary information
to the variance. Together, the VMR and skewness curves across scales form
a two-dimensional \emph{structural fingerprint} of the spatial
organization.

Scales are chosen as a logarithmic series from a minimum of 20~nm (set by
localization precision) to a maximum of $\mathrm{ROI}/4$ (ensuring
sufficient cells for reliable statistics), with the physical-to-voxel
conversion $s_\mathrm{vox} = \lfloor s_\mathrm{nm} / (L / G) \rfloor$
where $L$ is the ROI dimension. Duplicate scales after rounding are
removed. Typically 5--12 unique scales remain.

\subsection{Covariance-aware chi-squared test}

To assess whether the observed variance and skewness curves deviate from
the null expectation, we employ a covariance-aware chi-squared test. The
null distribution is established empirically by generating $M$ mock
datasets from a biological null model.

\subsubsection{Biological null model}

We construct null datasets by generating CSR within the convex hull of
the observed positions \citep{diggle2013}. The convex hull is computed
using Delaunay triangulation, and candidate points are drawn uniformly
within the bounding box and accepted only if they fall inside the hull
(rejection sampling). This preserves the overall spatial extent and
shape of the data while destroying any clustering structure. We note that
this assumes uniform background density; extension to inhomogeneous
Poisson null models that account for illumination gradients or membrane
curvature is an important direction for future work.

\subsubsection{Chi-squared statistic}

For a given statistic (variance or skewness), let
$\mathbf{y} \in \mathbb{R}^K$ be the curve from the real data and
$\{\mathbf{y}^{(m)}\}_{m=1}^{M}$ the curves from $M$ mock datasets,
where $K$ is the number of valid scales. The residual vector is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathbf{r} = \mathbf{y} - \bar{\mathbf{y}},
  \quad
  \bar{\mathbf{y}} = \frac{1}{M} \sum_{m=1}^{M} \mathbf{y}^{(m)}.
  \label{eq:residual}
\end{equation}

The sample covariance matrix $\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}$ is estimated
from the mock curves. To stabilize matrix inversion when $M$ is small
relative to $K$, we apply Ledoit--Wolf style shrinkage regularization
\citep{ledoit2004}:
\begin{equation}
  \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\mathrm{reg} =
    (1 - \lambda)\,\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}
    + \lambda\,\mathrm{diag}(\hat{\boldsymbol{\Sigma}}),
  \label{eq:shrinkage}
\end{equation}
where $\lambda \in [0, 1]$ is the shrinkage parameter. We use
$\lambda = 0.1$ by default, which maintains condition numbers around
$10^3$ for typical inputs (5--50 mocks, 5--12 scales). A small ridge
term ($10^{-10} \mathbf{I}$) is added for numerical stability.

The chi-squared statistic is:
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2 = \mathbf{r}^\top
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_\mathrm{reg}^{-1}\,\mathbf{r}.
  \label{eq:chi2}
\end{equation}

\subsection{Calibrated $p$-values}
\label{sec:calibrated-pvalues}

A naive $p$-value computed from the standard $\chi^2(K)$ reference
distribution is anti-conservative when the covariance matrix is estimated
from a finite number of mocks. To address this, we employ a corrected
chi-squared procedure based on the leave-one-out distribution of mock
chi-squared values.

For each mock $m$, we compute a leave-one-out chi-squared value by
treating mock $m$ as the ``real'' data and re-estimating the mean and
covariance from the remaining $M-1$ mocks:
\begin{equation}
  \chi^2_{\mathrm{LOO},m} =
    (\mathbf{y}^{(m)} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}_{-m})^\top\,
    \boldsymbol{\Sigma}_{\mathrm{reg},-m}^{-1}\,
    (\mathbf{y}^{(m)} - \bar{\mathbf{y}}_{-m}).
  \label{eq:loo}
\end{equation}

These leave-one-out values $\{\chi^2_{\mathrm{LOO},m}\}_{m=1}^{M}$
define the empirical null distribution. We fit a scaled chi-squared
distribution $a \cdot \chi^2(\nu)$ via method of moments:
\begin{equation}
  a = \frac{\mathrm{Var}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]}{2\,\mathrm{E}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]},
  \qquad
  \nu = \frac{2\,(\mathrm{E}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}])^2}
             {\mathrm{Var}[\chi^2_\mathrm{LOO}]}.
  \label{eq:moments}
\end{equation}

The corrected $p$-value for the real data is then:
\begin{equation}
  p = 1 - F_{\chi^2(\nu)}\!\left(\frac{\chi^2_\mathrm{real}}{a}\right),
  \label{eq:pvalue}
\end{equation}
where $F_{\chi^2(\nu)}$ is the CDF of the chi-squared distribution with
$\nu$ effective degrees of freedom. A minimum of 10 mocks is required for
stable moment estimation; with fewer mocks, $p = 1$ is returned.

\subsection{Detection decision rule}
\label{sec:detection-rule}

The test produces two $p$-values: $p_\mathrm{var}$ from the variance
curve and $p_\mathrm{skew}$ from the skewness curve. These capture
complementary aspects of clustering: variance reflects excess dispersion
(second moment), while skewness detects asymmetric count distributions
(third moment).

We employ an OR-combination rule: clustering is detected if either
$p_\mathrm{var} < \alpha$ or $p_\mathrm{skew} < \alpha$, where $\alpha$
is the per-test significance level.

\subsubsection{False positive rate under OR combination}

For two independent tests at significance level $\alpha$, the global
false positive rate under the OR rule is:
\begin{equation}
  \mathrm{FPR}_\mathrm{global} = 1 - (1 - \alpha)^2 \approx 2\alpha
  \quad \text{for small } \alpha.
  \label{eq:fpr_or}
\end{equation}

In practice, the variance and skewness statistics are not fully
independent (they are computed from the same density field), so the
actual inflation lies between $\alpha$ and $2\alpha$. Our empirical
calibration (Section~\ref{sec:exp-a}) confirms this.

\subsubsection{\v{S}id\'ak correction for strict control}

When strict control of the global FPR at a specified level
$\alpha_\mathrm{global}$ is required, the per-test threshold should be
set using the \v{S}id\'ak correction \citep{sidak1967}:
\begin{equation}
  \alpha_\mathrm{per} = 1 - (1 - \alpha_\mathrm{global})^{1/2}.
  \label{eq:sidak}
\end{equation}
For $\alpha_\mathrm{global} = 0.05$, this gives
$\alpha_\mathrm{per} \approx 0.0253$.

\subsection{Blinking correction}
\label{sec:blinking}

Fluorophore blinking in SMLM causes individual molecules to appear as
multiple localizations, artificially inflating apparent clustering. We
correct for this using connected-component merging with a UnionFind data
structure.

Two localizations are merged if they satisfy an anisotropic distance
criterion and a temporal proximity criterion:
\begin{equation}
  \left(\frac{\Delta x}{r_\mathrm{lat}}\right)^2 +
  \left(\frac{\Delta y}{r_\mathrm{lat}}\right)^2 +
  \left(\frac{\Delta z}{r_\mathrm{ax}}\right)^2 \leq 1
  \quad \text{and} \quad
  |\Delta f| \leq f_\mathrm{dark},
  \label{eq:blink}
\end{equation}
where $r_\mathrm{lat}$ and $r_\mathrm{ax}$ are the lateral and axial
merging radii (default 30 and 60~nm, respectively) and $f_\mathrm{dark}$
is the maximum number of dark frames between reappearances (default 5).
Merged localizations are replaced by their photon-weighted centroid.

\subsection{Baseline methods}
\label{sec:baselines}

We compare against three established methods, each calibrated with the
same mock datasets used for the multiscale test, ensuring a fair
comparison of the detection question (``is clustering present?'')
independently of cluster identification.

\subsubsection{DBSCAN}

DBSCAN \citep{ester1996} is run with $\varepsilon = 0.75 \times
r_\mathrm{cluster}$ and $\mathrm{min\_samples} = 5$. Detection is
determined by permutation calibration: the number of DBSCAN clusters in
the real data must exceed the 95th percentile of cluster counts from the
mock datasets. \textbf{Important caveat:} in synthetic experiments,
$r_\mathrm{cluster}$ is the known ground-truth cluster radius, giving
DBSCAN an informational advantage that would not be available in practice.
For experimental data, $r_\mathrm{cluster}$ is set to a user-specified
estimate (50~nm for NPC datasets, 30~nm for DNA-origami). Our multiscale
test, by contrast, requires no prior knowledge of cluster scale.

\subsubsection{HDBSCAN}

HDBSCAN addresses DBSCAN's fixed-$\varepsilon$ limitation by
automatically discovering clusters at varying densities. We use
$\mathrm{min\_cluster\_size} = 15$ with the scikit-learn implementation.
Detection uses the same permutation criterion as DBSCAN (cluster count
exceeding the 95th percentile of mock cluster counts). Unlike DBSCAN,
HDBSCAN requires no distance parameter, making it a fairer comparison
with our parameter-free multiscale test.

\subsubsection{Ripley's $L$ function}

The $L$ function is evaluated at 15 radii logarithmically spaced from
20 to 500~nm.
Detection is determined by envelope testing: the maximum deviation
$\max_r [L(r) - r]$ in the real data must exceed the 95th percentile of
the same statistic computed on the mock datasets.

\section{Results}

We present experimental results first, followed by synthetic validation
that characterizes the method's statistical properties in controlled
conditions.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Experimental data: detection and structural fingerprints}
\label{sec:exp-i}

\subsubsection{Datasets}

To validate the method on real SMLM data, we applied the multiscale
test to published 3D localization datasets from Heydarian et al.\
\citep{heydarian2021}, obtained from the 4TU.ResearchData repository.
These datasets contain individually segmented particles imaged with
STORM and DNA-PAINT techniques, with coordinates in camera pixel units
(converted to nanometers assuming 100~nm pixel size):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{NPC STORM}: 30 nuclear pore complex particles imaged with
    STORM. Nup107 protein localizations arranged in ring-shaped complexes
    with approximately 8-fold symmetry at $\sim$107~nm diameter.
  \item \textbf{NPC PAINT}: 25 nuclear pore complex particles imaged with
    DNA-PAINT. Same biological structure as above, different imaging
    modality.
  \item \textbf{DNA-origami tetrahedra}: 15 synthetic nanostructures with
    four precisely positioned vertices at designed locations.
\end{itemize}

We constructed realistic fields of view by placing multiple particles
at random positions within a $5000 \times 5000 \times 500$~nm ROI and
adding 2000 uniform background localizations to simulate non-specific
signal. Two negative controls were included: a synthetic CSR dataset
with the same total number of localizations as the STORM dataset, and a
permutation control created by randomizing the NPC STORM positions
uniformly within the same ROI (destroying spatial structure while
preserving density and geometry).

\subsubsection{Detection results}

The multiscale test was run with 15 mocks and default parameters.
DBSCAN ($\varepsilon = 37.5$~nm for NPC/CSR, $\varepsilon = 22.5$~nm
for tetrahedra, min\_samples~$= 5$), HDBSCAN
(min\_cluster\_size~$= 15$), and Ripley's $L$ function were run with
the same mock-based calibration (Table~\ref{tab:experimental}).

The multiscale test, DBSCAN, and Ripley's $L$ function all correctly
identify all three structured datasets and both negative controls.
HDBSCAN, despite being a more modern algorithm that does not require a
fixed distance parameter, performs poorly with the cluster-count-based
permutation calibration: it misses two of three structured datasets (NPC
PAINT, DNA-origami) and produces a false positive on the CSR control.
This occurs because HDBSCAN identifies density fluctuations as clusters
even in uniform random data, making the cluster-count statistic
unreliable for detection. This result illustrates a key advantage of our
approach: the multiscale test produces natively calibrated $p$-values,
whereas adapting cluster-identification algorithms for the detection
question requires method-specific tuning of the detection criterion.

\subsubsection{Structural fingerprints from experimental data}
\label{sec:fingerprints}

Beyond binary detection, the multiscale variance curves from the
experimental datasets reveal distinctive structural fingerprints
(Figure~\ref{fig:signatures}). The NPC datasets show a characteristic
variance excess at intermediate scales consistent with the $\sim$107~nm
ring diameter, while the DNA-origami tetrahedra produce a sharper
variance peak reflecting their smaller, more defined vertex clusters. The
CSR and permutation controls show flat variance curves consistent with
the mock envelope. These structural signatures are produced automatically
by the test and require no prior knowledge of the biological structure.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Multiscale signatures as diagnostic fingerprints}
\label{sec:exp-g}

To demonstrate that the multiscale curves encode morphological
information beyond binary detection, we examined the variance and
skewness signatures of four biologically motivated synthetic structures
(Figure~\ref{fig:signatures}):

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Synaptic receptors} (small, dense clusters at $r = 35$~nm
    \citep{macgillavry2013}): sharp variance peak at small scales,
    reflecting compact nanoclusters.
  \item \textbf{Nuclear pores} (ring-shaped at $r = 50$~nm
    \citep{loschberger2012}): characteristic variance peak at intermediate
    scales with a distinctive shape reflecting ring geometry.
  \item \textbf{Membrane domains} (large, sparse clusters at $r = 150$~nm
    \citep{owen2012}): broad variance excess extending across multiple
    scales.
  \item \textbf{Negative control} (pure CSR): variance curves remain
    within the mock envelope at all scales.
\end{itemize}

These distinct signatures suggest that the multiscale variance curve
acts as a \emph{structural fingerprint} of nanoscale molecular
organization. The peak scale of the variance excess encodes the
characteristic cluster size, the width of the excess reflects the
spatial extent of organization, and the skewness amplitude captures the
degree of asymmetry in the point distribution. This moves beyond the
binary ``clustered or not'' question toward a richer characterization of
\emph{what kind} of spatial organization is present---a question of
direct biological relevance when distinguishing, for example,
nanoclusters from membrane domains or scaffolded from diffusive
organization.

Developing a formal classification framework based on these fingerprints
is beyond the scope of this work but represents a promising direction.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Mixed-scale clustering}
\label{sec:mixed-scale}

Real biological systems often contain structure at multiple scales
simultaneously (e.g., receptor nanoclusters within larger membrane
domains). To test robustness to such hierarchical organization, we
generated synthetic data with both large domains ($r = 200$~nm,
$f = 0.10$) and small nanoclusters ($r = 30$~nm, $f = 0.15$) in the
same dataset.

The multiscale test correctly detects this mixed-scale clustering
($p < 0.001$ for both variance and skewness in all three replicates),
with the variance curve showing excess at both small and large scales.
DBSCAN requires choosing a single $\varepsilon$: at $\varepsilon = 22.5$~nm
it detects only 2--3 clusters (missing the large-scale structure), while
at $\varepsilon = 150$~nm it finds 66--87 clusters (merging and
fragmenting both scales). HDBSCAN and Ripley also detect the overall
clustering signal. However, only the multiscale variance curve reveals
the \emph{two-scale structure} of the organization, demonstrating the
diagnostic advantage of the fingerprint approach over methods that
produce a single detection statistic.

% ────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
\subsection{Synthetic validation}

The following experiments characterize the method's statistical
properties using synthetic data with known ground truth. Unless otherwise
specified, datasets contain 5000 molecules distributed within a
$5000 \times 5000 \times 500$~nm ROI, with Gaussian clusters of radius
75~nm, mean blinking rate of 5, lateral localization precision of 20~nm,
and 500 imaging frames. Grid size is $64^3$ and shrinkage
$\lambda = 0.1$.

\subsubsection{False positive rate calibration}
\label{sec:exp-a}

We generated 200 CSR datasets ($f = 0$) and applied the multiscale test
with 50 mocks each, using molecule positions (bypassing blinking
correction) to isolate statistical calibration from imaging artifacts.

Under a correctly calibrated null, $p$-values should follow a uniform
distribution on $[0, 1]$. The mean $p$-values were
$\bar{p}_\mathrm{var} = 0.464$ and $\bar{p}_\mathrm{skew} = 0.463$,
close to the expected value of 0.5 (Figure~\ref{fig:fpr}A).

$1 - (1 - 0.09)^2 = 17.2\%$ under independence
(Equation~\ref{eq:fpr_or}). The \v{S}id\'ak-corrected threshold
$\alpha_\mathrm{per} = 0.0253$ yields a combined FPR of 8.5\% (17/200).

DBSCAN with permutation calibration achieves FPR $= 0.0\%$
(conservative), while Ripley's envelope test yields FPR $= 8.5\%$ across
all thresholds.

\textbf{Pipeline FPR.} When simulated blinking and correction are
included, the pipeline FPR is 100\% at all significance levels. This
artifact arises from the blinking simulation model (see
Section~\ref{sec:discussion-limitations}), not the test itself. Our
experimental results (Section~\ref{sec:exp-i}) confirm correct behavior
on real data.

\textbf{Localization precision} (5--100~nm): TPR $= 1.0$.

\textbf{Blinking rate} (1--20 blinks/molecule, 10 replicates):
TPR $= 1.0$.

\textbf{Frame count} (20--2000): TPR $= 1.0$.

\section{Discussion}
\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Summary}

We have presented a multiscale statistical framework for SMLM data that
addresses three limitations of existing methods: (1)~single-scale
assumptions, by computing statistics across a logarithmic range of
scales; (2)~lack of formal calibration, by providing $p$-values via a
corrected chi-squared procedure; and (3)~arbitrary threshold choices, by
calibrating against a biological null model. Beyond detection, the
scale-dependent variance and skewness curves provide structural
fingerprints that characterize the spatial organization of molecular
assemblies.

\subsection{The method as a diagnostic test}

Our approach is designed as a \emph{diagnostic test}, not a segmentation
algorithm. It answers ``does clustering exist, and at what scales?''
rather than ``where are the clusters?'' This complements existing
cluster-identification tools: the multiscale test should be applied first
to determine whether there is statistically significant structure before
running DBSCAN, SR-Tesseler, or other segmentation methods whose outputs
are difficult to interpret when the data is consistent with CSR. The
structural fingerprint then provides guidance on which scales to target
for subsequent segmentation.

\subsection{Relationship to existing methods}

Our method occupies a distinct niche in the SMLM analysis landscape.
Compared to DBSCAN and HDBSCAN, which are designed for cluster
\emph{identification} and require careful parameter selection (or produce
unreliable detection when repurposed, as our HDBSCAN results
demonstrate), the multiscale test is designed specifically for the
\emph{detection} question and produces calibrated $p$-values natively.
Compared to Ripley's $L$ function, which also addresses detection, our
method provides richer scale-dependent information through the full
variance and skewness curves rather than a single summary statistic, and
shows higher sensitivity at low cluster fractions.

Voronoi-based methods (SR-Tesseler, ClusterViSu) and machine-learning
approaches (CAML, ClusterNet) operate in the segmentation domain and
would be natural downstream tools after our test identifies significant
clustering. A formal comparison with these methods would require
adapting them to the detection question---an interesting direction but
beyond the scope of this work.

\subsection{Strengths}

The method provides calibrated per-test $p$-values (9.0\% FPR at
$\alpha = 0.05$ from 200 null datasets, compared to the nominal 5\%),
is robust across all tested parameters, handles diverse cluster
geometries including elongated and ring-shaped structures, and
generalizes to experimental data from multiple imaging modalities. The
HDBSCAN comparison is particularly informative: despite being a modern
algorithm that automatically discovers clusters at varying densities,
HDBSCAN misses 2/3 of structured experimental datasets and produces a
false positive on CSR when using cluster-count-based permutation
calibration. This underscores the value of methods that produce natively
calibrated statistical outputs.

The structural fingerprint capability adds biological interpretability
beyond binary detection, connecting the method to the broader question of
``what kind of nanoscale organization exists?'' rather than just ``is
clustering present?''

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{sec:discussion-limitations}

\subsubsection{Per-test FPR elevation}

The per-test FPR of 9.0\% at $\alpha = 0.05$ exceeds the nominal level.
This is attributable to two factors: (1) finite-sample bias in the
corrected chi-squared calibration (the leave-one-out procedure uses only
$M-1$ mocks for each calibration, introducing variance in the moment
estimates), and (2) partial correlation between the variance and skewness
statistics computed from the same density field. The OR-combination
further inflates the global FPR to 16.0\%. Users requiring strict control
should apply the \v{S}id\'ak correction, which reduces the combined FPR
to 8.5\%.

\subsubsection{Pipeline FPR from blinking simulation}

When simulated blinking and correction are included, the pipeline FPR
reaches 100\%. The root cause is that with 500 imaging frames and a
dark-frame window of 5 frames, the temporal merging probability is only
$\sim$2\%, leaving each molecule as $\sim$5 separate localizations
clustered within $\sim$15~nm. These residual mini-clusters represent
genuine non-CSR structure. This is specific to the simulation model;
our experimental results confirm correct behavior on real data. We
recommend validating the pipeline on experimental negative controls when
available.

\subsubsection{Detection without localization}

The method detects clustering and characterizes its scale structure but
does not identify individual clusters or assign localizations to groups.
Users requiring spatial cluster assignments should apply the multiscale
test first for detection, then use DBSCAN, SR-Tesseler, or other
segmentation tools---guided by the peak scale of the variance curve---for
cluster identification.

\subsubsection{Convex hull null model}

The biological null model assumes uniform density within the convex hull.
In real experiments, spatially varying background (illumination gradients,
membrane curvature) could violate this assumption. Extension to
inhomogeneous Poisson null models is an important future direction.

\subsubsection{Choice of summary statistics}

The test uses two statistics: variance (second moment) and skewness
(third moment) of the count distribution. While these capture the
dominant deviations from CSR, higher-order cumulants (kurtosis, etc.)
could potentially detect additional structure. We chose variance and
skewness because they provide a good balance of sensitivity and stability
with finite data; the geometry robustness results (Gaussian, elongated,
ring-shaped clusters all detected at TPR $= 1.0$) suggest these two
moments are sufficient for common biological clustering patterns.

\subsubsection{Experimental data scope}

Our experimental validation uses three datasets from a single source
(Heydarian et al.\ \citep{heydarian2021}). While these span two imaging
modalities (STORM, DNA-PAINT) and two biological structures (protein
complexes, synthetic nanostructures), broader validation on diverse
receptor clustering datasets, membrane protein distributions, and data
from independent laboratories would further establish generalizability.

\subsection{Future work}

\begin{itemize}
  \item \textbf{Structural classification}: developing a formal
    classification framework based on the multiscale fingerprints
    (peak scale, width, skewness amplitude) to automatically
    distinguish biological organization types.
  \item \textbf{Inhomogeneous null models}: extending the null model to
    account for spatially varying background density.
  \item \textbf{Broader experimental validation}: application to
    receptor clustering (TCR, EGFR), membrane domain, and
    independently characterized datasets.
  \item \textbf{Larger FPR calibration}: 500+ null datasets for tighter
    confidence intervals.
  \item \textbf{2D extension}: optimized variant for 2D-only
    localization data.
  \item \textbf{GPU acceleration}: parallelized CIC gridding and
    mock generation for datasets exceeding $10^5$ localizations.
\end{itemize}

\section{Reproducibility}

All source code and results are available at
\url{https://github.com/saivoru777-ship-it/smlm-multiscale-clustering}.
Experimental datasets are from Heydarian et al.\ \citep{heydarian2021}
(4TU.ResearchData, doi:10.4121/13797686). The validation study can be
reproduced with:

\begin{verbatim}
# Full study (9 experiments, ~6 hours)
python3 -m smlm_clustering.validation.comprehensive_study \
    --output-dir results/final

# Quick mode (~1-2 hours, reduced replicates)
python3 -m smlm_clustering.validation.comprehensive_study \
    --quick --output-dir results/final

# Regenerate figures from existing results
python3 -m smlm_clustering.validation.comprehensive_study \
    --figures-only --output-dir results/final \
    --figures-dir figures
\end{verbatim}

Results are stored as JSON files. Figures use the Wong colorblind-safe
palette \citep{wong2011}.
