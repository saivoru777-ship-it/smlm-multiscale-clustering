We are pleased to submit our manuscript, "A multiscale covariance-aware
statistical test for clustering in single-molecule localization
microscopy," for consideration in [Journal Name].

Single-molecule localization microscopy (SMLM) has become a standard
tool for studying molecular organization at the nanoscale. However,
existing clustering analysis methods (DBSCAN, Ripley's K, Voronoi
tessellation) typically operate at a single spatial scale and lack
formal null hypothesis testing with calibrated p-values, making it
difficult to distinguish genuine clustering from stochastic fluctuations
at controlled significance levels.

Our manuscript presents a multiscale statistical test that addresses
these limitations by:

1. Computing variance and skewness of counts-in-cells across a
   logarithmic range of spatial scales, capturing clustering signatures
   from tens to hundreds of nanometers simultaneously.

2. Quantifying deviations from a biological null model using a
   covariance-aware chi-squared statistic with shrinkage regularization,
   producing calibrated p-values via a corrected chi-squared procedure.

3. Validating the method through eight comprehensive experiments
   covering false positive rate calibration (200 null datasets with
   binomial confidence intervals), sensitivity analysis, parameter
   robustness, geometry robustness, realistic imaging conditions,
   and biological use cases.

Key findings include: the test detects clustering at lower signal
levels (cluster fraction >= 0.03) than DBSCAN (>= 0.10) and Ripley's
L function (>= 0.05); detection is robust across cluster geometries,
localization precisions, and blinking rates; and a negative control
is correctly not detected. We transparently report both the strengths
and the limitations of the method, including the false positive rate
inflation from combining multiple test statistics.

The manuscript includes all source code and results for full
reproducibility.

We believe this work will be of interest to the computational biology
and bioimaging communities as a rigorous statistical framework for
SMLM clustering analysis.
